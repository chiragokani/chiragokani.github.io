Explaining the tone of two legendary jazz guitarists: sonifications

To study the tone of jazz guitarists Wes Montgomery and Joe Pass, we modeled the excitation of a stiff and lossy guitar string by the thumb, finger, and pick. The spectrum of frequencies generated by exciting the string is characterized by the root-mean-squared bandwidth, \(\beta_\mathrm{rms}\), the values of which are shown below as the red numbers for various positions excitation \(0.70 \leq D \leq 0.85\) (where \(D= 0\) corresponds to the nut and \(D=1\) corresponds to the bridge) and amounts of slipping \(0 \leq \Delta \leq 1/\sqrt2\) (where \(\Delta = 0\) corresponds to pure striking and \(\Delta = 1/\sqrt{2}\) corresponds to pure plucking). The parameters in red boxes correspond approximately to the combination of \(D\) and \(\Delta\) that describe Montgomery and Pass. The black-to-white gradient corresponds to the values \(1 \leq \beta_\mathrm{rms} \leq 3.456\). Click on each number to hear the corresponding sound.
